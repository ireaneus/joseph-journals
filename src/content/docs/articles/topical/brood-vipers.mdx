## Spiritual Abortion

Many professing Christians condemn abortion, and rightly so — Scripture knows the child in the womb (Psalm 139:13), and the church has defended that life against every slogan the world invented to end it. But some of these same Christians preach a doctrine that performs the same procedure on the soul. It teaches that Christ's work restored every sinner to a neutral place, where the dead man himself decides whether he will live. It sounds compassionate. It aborts the entire work of the Holy Spirit in salvation and leaves the sinner exactly where it found him — dead in his sins, now with the added burden of believing the choice was his to make.

The doctrine is called prevenient grace, and Paul had a category for gospels that hand salvation back to the sinner:

> *"I am amazed that you are so quickly deserting Him who called you by the grace of Christ, for a different gospel, which is not just another account; but there are some who are disturbing you and want to distort the gospel of Christ. But even if we, or an angel from heaven, should preach to you a gospel contrary to what we have preached to you, he is to be accursed!"*  
> **—Galatians 1:6-8**

Strong words demand honest definitions. So define the terms first — each from the tradition that holds it — and then test the doctrine against the Scriptures.

## Defining the Terms

**Prevenient grace** ("preventing" in the older English, meaning *going before*) is the Wesleyan/Arminian teaching that God extends a universal, enabling grace to every human being — sufficient to counteract the deadness of the fall and restore in every person the ability to accept or reject the gospel. The term itself comes from the Anglican Articles of Religion, which John Wesley retained for the Methodists:

> *"The condition of Man after the fall of Adam is such, that he cannot turn and prepare himself, by his own natural strength and good works, to faith; and calling upon God. Wherefore we have no power to do good works pleasant and acceptable to God, without the grace of God by Christ preventing us, that we may have a good will, and working with us, when we have that good will."*  
> — **Articles of Religion, Article X, "Of Free-Will" (1571)**

Note carefully what the Article actually confesses: man *cannot turn himself*. The Wesleyan system takes that grace which "prevents us" and universalizes it — teaching, as Wesley argued in his sermon "On Working Out Our Own Salvation," that a measure of this enabling grace is given to every person without exception, so that no sinner is finally unable to respond.

**Irresistible grace** (better termed *effectual* grace) is the Reformed teaching that when God purposes to save a sinner, His Spirit actually accomplishes it — regenerating the dead heart so that the sinner comes willingly, and comes certainly. The Synod of Dort (1618–19) confessed it this way:

> *"According to which decree He graciously softens the hearts of the elect, however obstinate, and inclines them to believe."*  
> — **Canons of Dort, First Head of Doctrine, Article 6**

The difference between the two doctrines is the whole argument of this article: prevenient grace makes salvation possible; effectual grace saves.

## Dead Means Dead

Give the honest Wesleyan his due — he confesses total depravity. His own Article X says man cannot turn himself. On the diagnosis, we agree entirely. The disagreement is over what God did about it, and here the Scriptures speak in the language of a morgue:

> *"And you were dead in your offenses and sins, in which you previously walked according to the course of this world."*  
> **—Ephesians 2:1-2**

> *"There is no righteous person, not even one;"*
> *"There is no one who understands,"*
> *"There is no one who seeks out God;"*
> *"They have all turned aside, together they have become corrupt;"*
> *"There is no one who does good,"*
> *"There is not even one.”*  
> **—Romans 3:10-12**

Dead is not sick. Dead is not weakened. Dead is not neutral. A corpse does not cooperate with its resurrection, and the natural man cannot even comprehend the offer being made to him:

> *"But a natural man does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are foolishness to him; and he cannot understand them, because they are spiritually appraised."*  
> **—1 Corinthians 2:14**

Cannot. Prevenient grace answers this by proposing that God quietly repealed the *cannot* for every person on earth. If that were true, Scripture would say so somewhere. So look at the verses where its defenders say it does.

## Their Strongest Texts, Examined

**"I will draw all men to Myself" (John 12:32).** The claim: Christ's cross exerts a universal drawing that every sinner may resist. But watch the context — Greeks had just come seeking Jesus (John 12:20-21), and His answer is that the cross will draw not Jews only but all men without distinction: every tribe, tongue, people, and nation. And John has already defined what the Father's drawing accomplishes:

> *"No one can come to Me unless the Father who sent Me draws him; and I will raise him up on the last day."*  
> **—John 6:44**

The drawing of John 6:44 ends in resurrection. Everyone drawn is raised. That is not a universal invitation that mostly fails; it is an effectual summons that cannot.

**"The grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation to all men" (Titus 2:11).** The claim: saving grace has been dispensed to every individual. But read the verse — the grace that appeared *brings salvation*, not the possibility of salvation. And Paul has just walked through the household: older men, older women, young men, bondslaves (Titus 2:1-10). Grace bringing salvation to *all men* — every kind of person, every station, every class. The verse proves the reach of grace, and it proves too much for the Wesleyan: if this grace were given to every individual, every individual would be saved, because this grace *brings salvation*.

**"The kindness of God leads you to repentance" (Romans 2:4).** The claim: God's universal kindness enables every sinner to repent. But common grace restrains sin and sustains moral agency **(Romans 2:14-15)** — it does not raise the dead. Paul's very point in Romans 2 is that men treasure up wrath *despite* the kindness. Kindness leads; only grace grants:

> *"The kindness of God leads you to repentance."*  
> **—Romans 2:4**

Repentance itself is a gift God grants **(2 Timothy 2:25)** — which is precisely what prevenient grace denies when it makes repentance the one contribution the sinner supplies on his own.

Three texts, and in each case the doctrine has to be carried *to* the verse, because it cannot be found *in* it.

## Brood of Vipers

Jesus reserved His hottest words for zealous evangelists with a corrupt message:

> *"Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you travel around on sea and land to make one proselyte; and when he becomes one, you make him twice as much a son of hell as yourselves."*  
> **—Matthew 23:15**

Mark what Jesus did not question. He did not question their zeal — it carried them over sea and land. He did not question their sincerity, their effort, or their results. He questioned their gospel. A message that leaves the sinner trusting his own religious performance produces converts, and it produces them twice as lost — confirmed in a righteousness that cannot save. And to the men preaching it, He said:

> *"You serpents, you brood of vipers, how will you escape the sentence of hell?"*  
> **—Matthew 23:33**

Here is the hypocrisy this article is named for. The world justifies abortion with "my body, my choice." The preacher of prevenient grace stands in the pulpit and offers the sinner "my will, my choice" — and does not hear the echo. One severs a life from the body that carries it; the other severs salvation from the God who accomplishes it, and hands the dead man the credit for his own resurrection. The same church that marches against the first slogan fills its altar calls with the second.

## The True Gospel of Life

The true Gospel declares that salvation is the work of God — beginning, middle, and end.

> *"No one can come to Me unless the Father who sent Me draws him; and I will raise him up on the last day."*  
> **—John 6:44**

> *"It is the Spirit who gives life; the flesh profits nothing; the words that I have spoken to you are spirit and are life... For this reason I have said to you, that no one can come to Me unless it has been granted him from the Father."*  
> **—John 6:63, 65**

And when John describes those who receive Christ, he traces their birth to its source with three doors slammed shut and one left open:

> *"But as many as received Him, to them He gave the right to become children of God, even to those who believe in His name, who were born, not of blood nor of the will of the flesh nor of the will of man, but of God."*  
> **—John 1:12-13**

Not of the will of man. Born of God. The order of the true Gospel stands:

- God chooses and calls: salvation begins with God’s election **(Ephesians 1:4-5)**.
- The Spirit regenerates: the Holy Spirit brings spiritual life to the dead **(Titus 3:5)**.
- Christ accomplished redemption: His death and resurrection secure salvation for His people **(Hebrews 9:12; Luke 1:68)**.
- The sinner repents and believes: the first breath of a heart already made alive — a broken and contrite response to effectual grace **(Psalm 51)**.

## Conclusion: Examine Your Heart

Paul’s warning in Galatians 1 could not be clearer: *“If anyone is preaching to you a gospel contrary to the one you received, let him be accursed”* **(Galatians 1:9)**.

He also commands us to examine ourselves: *“Test yourselves to see if you are in the faith; examine yourselves! Or do you not recognize this about yourselves, that Jesus Christ is in you—unless indeed you fail the test?”* **(2 Corinthians 13:5)**.

So examine your heart. If your confidence rests anywhere in the strength of your own choosing, you are holding fool's confidence in a dead man's will. But if there is in you a broken and contrite heart crying, *“Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a steadfast spirit within me”* **(Psalm 51:10)** — that cry did not come from a neutral will weighing its options. Dead men do not cry out. That cry is the first breath of a heart God has already made alive.

Ask Ezekiel what dry bones contribute to their own rattling. Then give the glory where the breath came from.
