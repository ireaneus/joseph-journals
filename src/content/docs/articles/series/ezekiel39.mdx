## Coming Out of Chapter 38

Chapter 38 ended with the Lord's fury fully displayed — a planet-wide earthquake, the armies of Gog turned against one another, torrential rain, hailstones, fire and brimstone descending on an invading force that never reached its objective. Israel did nothing. The Lord fought the battle entirely on their behalf, and He stated His purpose plainly:

> **Ezekiel 38:23 (NASB1995)**
> *"I will magnify Myself, sanctify Myself, and make Myself known in the sight of many nations; and they will know that I am the LORD."*

Chapter 39 does not begin a new subject. It restates the opening circumstances of the war — who Gog is, where he came from, how the Lord brought him — and then moves the narrative into a new direction: the aftermath. What happens after the defeat of Gog is not a footnote. It is the chapter's primary concern, because it is in the aftermath that the Lord accomplishes the second great purpose of the war: making Himself known to the Gentile nations of the earth as the God who dwells in the midst of Israel.

Looking at the structure of Ezekiel 33–48, Chapter 38 belonged to the third covenant promise — a people dwelling in peace. Chapter 39 belongs to the fourth — the Lord dwelling among His people. The war serves both. Chapter 38 proves the covenant of peace by defending it. Chapter 39 declares the Lord's glory to the nations so that what follows in Chapters 40–48 — the restored temple, the returned glory, the Lord dwelling with Israel forever — is received by a world that has just witnessed His power and cannot pretend ignorance.

## The Battle Restated — and Quickly Ended

> **Ezekiel 39:1-5 (NASB1995)**
> *"And you, son of man, prophesy against Gog and say, 'Thus says the Lord GOD, "Behold, I am against you, O Gog, prince of Rosh, Meshech and Tubal; and I will turn you around, drive you on, take you up from the remotest parts of the north and bring you against the mountains of Israel. I will strike your bow from your left hand and dash down your arrows from your right hand. You will fall on the mountains of Israel, you and all your troops and the peoples who are with you; I will give you as food to every kind of predatory bird and beast of the field. You will fall on the open field; for it is I who have spoken," declares the Lord GOD.'"*

The chapter opens with a brief restatement of the war's origin and outcome. The Lord reiterates that He brought Gog against the mountains of Israel — this is His war, ordained by His sovereign will — and then describes the defeat in a single gesture: He strikes the bow from Gog's left hand and the arrows from his right. The army falls on the mountains of Israel before it reaches its objective. What began as an uncountable host covering the ground like a cloud ends as a field of the slain.

The weapons are bow and arrow. Chapter 38 mentioned shields, bucklers, and swords. Chapter 39 will confirm they are made of wood. In a thousand years of Kingdom peace, the art of metallurgical warfare has been entirely lost. An army armed with wooden tools advancing against a nation under the direct protection of the Lord is — as one might observe of the people of Babel building a tower to reach heaven — a spectacle of human arrogance colliding with divine omnipotence.

> **Ezekiel 39:6 (NASB1995)**
> *"And I will send fire upon Magog and those who inhabit the coastlands in safety; and they will know that I am the LORD."*

The Lord does not limit His response to the invading army on the mountains of Israel. Fire goes out to Magog itself — the homeland from which the army came — and to the coastlands. The judgment is comprehensive. No nation that participated remains untouched. And the stated purpose is the same refrain that has driven the entire war: *they will know that I am the LORD.*

## The Lord's Name Made Known in Israel

> **Ezekiel 39:7-8 (NASB1995)**
> *"My holy name I will make known in the midst of My people Israel; and I will not let My holy name be profaned anymore. And the nations will know that I am the LORD, the Holy One in Israel. Behold, it is coming and it shall be done," declares the Lord GOD. "That is the day of which I have spoken."*

A question worth pausing over: if this is the millennial Kingdom, and all Israel knows the Lord — as Jeremiah's new covenant promise guarantees — then why does the Lord say He will make His name known *in the midst of My people Israel*?

> **Jeremiah 31:33-34 (NASB1995)**
> *"But this is the covenant which I will make with the house of Israel after those days," declares the LORD, "I will put My law within them and on their heart I will write it; and I will be their God, and they shall be My people. They will not teach again, each man his neighbor and each man his brother, saying, 'Know the LORD,' for they will all know Me, from the least of them to the greatest of them."*

The answer is in the audience. The Lord is not saying that Israel needs to be reminded of who He is. He is saying that He will make Himself known *in the sight of the nations* — through what He does in the midst of Israel. The Gentile nations observing this event are the audience He is addressing. The stage is Israel. The testimony is the war's outcome. And the declaration goes outward from Israel to every nation watching.

This matters because in the Kingdom, unbelief is still possible. Natural men and women continue to be born, live, and die with original sin. Faith is still required of them — confidence in what is not seen. Jesus reigns from the temple; He is not visible in the streets. The word of God is still the evidence that must be trusted. And in that context, the Lord's dramatic defeat of Gog — executed publicly, on a plain visible to the watching world — becomes an unmistakable testimony that the God of Israel is real, present, and faithful.

> **Psalm 110:1-2 (NASB1995)**
> *"The LORD says to my Lord: 'Sit at My right hand until I make Your enemies a footstool for Your feet.' The LORD will stretch forth Your strong scepter from Zion, saying, 'Rule in the midst of Your enemies!'"*

This is the day the Lord has spoken of. The day Psalm 110 anticipated. The day Christ's final enemy is placed under His feet — and every nation on earth sees it happen.

## Seven Years of Burning

> **Ezekiel 39:9-10 (NASB1995)**
> *"Then those who inhabit the cities of Israel will go out and make fires with the weapons and burn them, both shields and bucklers, bows and arrows, war clubs and spears, and for seven years they will make fires of them. They will not take wood from the field or gather firewood from the forests, for they will make fires with the weapons; and they will take the spoil of those who despoiled them and seize the plunder of those who plundered them," declares the Lord GOD.*

The people of Israel emerge from their unwalled villages — where they had remained safely throughout the entire battle — and begin collecting the weapons of the fallen army. What they find is so vast in quantity that it will supply all of Israel with fuel for burning for seven full years. They will not need to gather wood from forests or fields. The weapons of the invaders become the provision of the defended.

This detail is one of the most decisive timing markers in the entire passage. If this war occurred at the end of the Tribulation period — as the third interpretive view holds — there would be no room for seven years of burning afterward. The Tribulation itself is seven years, and Daniel 12 places the cleanup of Tribulation's destruction within seventy-five days of its close. There is simply no window for an additional seven-year burning that follows.

> **Daniel 12:11-12 (NASB1995)**
> *"From the time that the regular sacrifice is abolished and the abomination of desolation is set up, there will be 1,290 days. How blessed is he who keeps waiting and attains to the 1,335 days!"*

But if the war occurs after the thousand years are completed — as Revelation 20 places it — then the seven years of burning extends beyond the formal Kingdom period without contradiction, because the Kingdom itself is not bounded by a hard stop at the thousand-year mark. The peace of the Kingdom lasts a thousand years. The war follows. The aftermath follows the war. There is no time constraint that cannot accommodate it.

And for those who would place the war in our present age: no modern nation collecting the debris of a defeated army would burn wooden weapons for seven years as a fuel source. Modern Israel heats with natural gas. Its cities run on electricity. The detail only makes sense in the simpler, wood-dependent economy of the Kingdom age — where Isaiah confirms that animals pull transportation and the implements of war have been beaten into farming tools for a thousand years.

> **Isaiah 9:5 (NASB1995)**
> *"For every boot of the booted warrior in the battle tumult, and cloak rolled in blood, will be for burning, fuel for the fire."*

Isaiah saw it centuries before Ezekiel wrote it. The debris of this battle becomes fuel. The word of God compiles without contradiction when the text is allowed to speak for itself.

## Seven Months of Burial — Cleansing the Land

> **Ezekiel 39:11-16 (NASB1995)**
> *"On that day I will give Gog a burial ground there in Israel, the valley of those who pass by east of the sea, and it will block off those who would pass by. So they will bury Gog there with all his horde, and they will call it the valley of Hamon-gog. For seven months the house of Israel will be burying them in order to cleanse the land. Even all the people of the land will bury them; and it will be to their renown on the day that I glorify Myself," declares the Lord GOD. "They will set apart men who will constantly pass through the land, burying those who were passing through, even those left on the surface of the ground, in order to cleanse it. At the end of seven months they will make a search. As those who pass through the land pass through and anyone sees a man's bone, then he will set up a marker by it until the buriers have buried it in the valley of Hamon-gog. And even the name of the city will be Hamonah. So they will cleanse the land."*

The sheer scale of the fallen army is conveyed by what the burial requires. Every person in Israel participates. A dedicated team of men does nothing but search the land for unburied bones, marking each one carefully so that no Jew inadvertently passes over it and becomes ceremonially defiled. The process takes seven months. The burial ground — named Hamon-gog, meaning *the multitude of Gog* — fills an entire valley east of the Mediterranean, east of the sea, in what is most naturally identified as the Jordan River valley. The bodies are so numerous that the burial ground blocks passage through the valley.

The care Israel takes in burying the bodies of those who came to destroy her is itself a testimony. It is not the response of a people celebrating a military victory they won by their own strength. It is the response of a people who know they were defended by their God, who handle even the bodies of enemies with the reverence the Lord requires, and who labor for seven months to restore the purity of their land.

The Lord gives the reason in verse 13:

> **Ezekiel 39:13 (NASB1995)**
> *"Even all the people of the land will bury them; and it will be to their renown on the day that I glorify Myself."*

Israel's careful, patient, gracious response to this unprecedented event will bring them renown among all the nations. The Gentile world watching Israel bury her enemies with such order and dignity will see a people unlike any other — and will know that the God who is with them is holy.

> **Jeremiah 33:9 (NASB1995)**
> *"It will be to Me a name of joy, praise and glory before all the nations of the earth which will hear of all the good that I do for them, and they will fear and tremble because of all the good and all the peace that I make for it."*

> **Zephaniah 3:19-20 (NASB1995)**
> *"Behold, I am going to deal at that time with all your oppressors, I will save the lame and gather the outcast, and I will turn their shame into praise and renown in all the earth. At that time I will bring you in, even at the time when I gather you together; indeed, I will give you renown and praise among all the peoples of the earth, when I restore your fortunes before your eyes," says the LORD.*

## The Lord's Sacrifice — The Final Offering

> **Ezekiel 39:17-20 (NASB1995)**
> *"As for you, son of man, thus says the Lord GOD, 'Speak to every kind of bird and to every beast of the field, "Assemble and come, gather from every side to My sacrifice which I am going to sacrifice for you, as a great sacrifice on the mountains of Israel, that you may eat flesh and drink blood. You will eat the flesh of mighty men and drink the blood of the princes of the earth, as though they were rams, lambs, goats and bulls, all of them fatlings of Bashan. So you will eat fat until you are glutted, and drink blood until you are drunk, from My sacrifice which I have sacrificed for you. You will be glutted at My table with horses and charioteers, with mighty men and all the men of war," declares the Lord GOD.'"*

The language here is striking and intentional. Throughout Scripture, sacrifices are made by men — animals offered to God as a covering for sin, a picture pointing forward to the once-for-all sacrifice of Christ. Now, in this final act, the roles are reversed. The Lord makes the sacrifice. The offering is not an animal but those who came against His people. The beneficiaries are the birds and the beasts of the field.

This is the Lord declaring that the need for sacrifice is finished. The Lamb of God has already been offered, once for all:

> **Hebrews 9:11-12 (NASB1995)**
> *"But when Christ appeared as a high priest of the good things to come, He entered through the greater and more perfect tabernacle, not made with hands, that is to say, not of this creation; and not through the blood of goats and calves, but through His own blood, He entered the holy place once for all, having obtained eternal redemption."*

> **Hebrews 10:12-14 (NASB1995)**
> *"But He, having offered one sacrifice for sins for all time, sat down at the right hand of God, waiting from that point onward until His enemies be made a footstool for His feet. For by one offering He has perfected for all time those who are sanctified."*

In this final moment of the age, the Lord makes the last offering — not for atonement, but for judgment. Those who opposed Him become the sacrifice. And in that act, the Lord closes the age of warfare, rebellion, and the reign of death. The enemy is gone. The sacrifice is made. The purpose of the Kingdom is fulfilled.

Some draw a connection between this passage and Revelation 19, where birds are likewise called to feast on the fallen at Armageddon:

> **Revelation 19:17-18 (NASB1995)**
> *"Then I saw an angel standing in the sun, and he cried out with a loud voice, saying to all the birds which fly in midheaven, 'Come, assemble for the great supper of God, so that you may eat the flesh of kings and the flesh of commanders and the flesh of mighty men and the flesh of horses and of those who sit on them and the flesh of all men, both free men and slaves, and small and great.'"*

The parallel is real but the events are distinct. Armageddon concludes the Tribulation. Gog's war concludes the Kingdom. Both involve massive loss of life and birds feasting on the fallen — but the context, timing, combatants, and purpose of each are different. To collapse them into one event requires overriding the plain reading of Revelation 20, which places the release of Satan, the deception of Gog and Magog, and the surrounding of the beloved city *after* the thousand years are completed. The text does not permit the merger.

## The Lord Sets His Glory Among the Nations

> **Ezekiel 39:21-22 (NASB1995)**
> *"And I will set My glory among the nations; and all the nations will see My judgment which I have executed and My hand which I have laid on them. And the house of Israel will know that I am the LORD their God from that day onward."*

This is the culminating purpose of the entire Gog war, stated plainly. The Lord sets His glory among the nations. Every nation on earth — those who watched the invasion gather, those who observed the defeat, those who heard what happened — will see the judgment the Lord executed and the hand He laid on the invaders. The Lord who dwells in the temple in Jerusalem, unseen, has acted in the open field in a way that cannot be explained by any power other than His own.

And Israel — who lived securely through the entire event, who emerged to find the invaders slain on the mountains around them, who spent seven months burying the dead and seven years burning the weapons — will know from that day forward, with a depth and finality they had not known before, that the LORD is their God. Not because they had forgotten — they had not. But because they had now seen, in the most dramatic possible terms, what it means to be the people whose God is the LORD.

## The Nations Come to Understand Israel's History

> **Ezekiel 39:23-24 (NASB1995)**
> *"The nations will know that the house of Israel went into exile for their iniquity because they acted treacherously against Me, and I hid My face from them; so I gave them into the hand of their adversaries, and all of them fell by the sword. According to their uncleanness and according to their transgressions I dealt with them, and I hid My face from them."*

This is a remarkable statement. Through the defeat of Gog, the nations will not only see the Lord's power in the present. They will understand Israel's past. They will come to comprehend that Israel's centuries of exile, scattering, persecution, and apparent abandonment were not evidence of a weak God or a failed covenant. They were the Lord's righteous judgment on a people who acted treacherously against Him. He hid His face — not because He was absent, but because He is holy and just, and the consequences of covenant-breaking are real.

This reframes everything the nations had ever thought about Israel. The Jewish people scattered among the nations did not look like the people of a powerful God. They looked like the people of a defeated one. But through the events of the Gog war and its aftermath, the Lord declares the true interpretation of that history: every adversary that prevailed over Israel did so only because the Lord permitted it. He gave them into the hand of their adversaries. He was in control — even then. Even in the exile. Even in the silence.

> **Ezekiel 39:29 (NASB1995)**
> *"I will not hide My face from them any longer, for I will have poured out My Spirit on the house of Israel," declares the Lord GOD.*

The hiding is over. The Spirit has been poured out. The nation has been restored. And what follows — the rebuilt temple, the returned glory, the Lord dwelling among His people in Chapters 40–48 — is the permanent, unending state that these events have now brought about in full view of every nation on earth.

## The Lord Restores the Fortunes of Jacob

> **Ezekiel 39:25-29 (NASB1995)**
> *"Therefore thus says the Lord GOD, 'Now I will restore the fortunes of Jacob and have mercy on the whole house of Israel; and I will be jealous for My holy name. They will forget their disgrace and all their treachery which they perpetrated against Me, when they live securely on their own land with no one to make them afraid. When I bring them back from the peoples and gather them from the lands of their enemies, then I shall be sanctified through them in the sight of the many nations. Then they will know that I am the LORD their God because I made them go into exile among the nations, and then gathered them again to their own land; and I will leave none of them there any longer. I will not hide My face from them any longer, for I will have poured out My Spirit on the house of Israel,' declares the Lord GOD."*

This closing summary is the Lord's own account of the entire arc — from exile to restoration to the Kingdom to this final defeat of the last enemy. He restores the fortunes of Jacob. He has mercy on the whole house of Israel. He is jealous for His holy name. And Israel, living securely on their own land with no one left to make them afraid, will forget their disgrace.

The forgetting is not denial. It is the mercy of God applied so completely that what was once a source of shame — the exile, the scattering, the centuries of reproach — is swallowed up in the fullness of what the Lord has done. The Lord's faithfulness is so comprehensive, so undeniable, and so publicly demonstrated that the shame cannot survive in its presence.

The nations will be sanctified through Israel. Israel will know the Lord as never before. And the Lord will no longer hide His face — because the Spirit has been poured out, the covenant fulfilled, the enemies defeated, and the age of warfare closed.

> **Isaiah 55:11 (NASB1995)**
> *"So will My word be which goes forth from My mouth; it will not return to Me empty, without accomplishing what I desire, and without succeeding in the matter for which I sent it."*

## The Bridge to Chapters 40–48

Ezekiel 39 is not the end of the book. It is the threshold. Everything from Chapter 33 to this point has been building toward what follows — the Lord dwelling among His people, not as a promise still outstanding, but as the present and permanent reality.

Chapter 38 proved the covenant of peace by defending it. Chapter 39 declared the Lord's glory to every nation on earth through the aftermath of the war. Now, with no enemy remaining, no competing claim on the land, no scattering, no exile, no hiding of the Lord's face — the book moves into the detailed description of the restored temple, the returned glory, the priestly order, the allotment of the land, and the name that closes the entire book:

> *"The LORD is there."* **(Ezekiel 48:35 NASB1995)**

That name — *the LORD is there* — is the destination of every chapter from Ezekiel 1 onward. The glory that departed in Chapter 10 returns in Chapter 43. The God who said *I will be their God and they will be My people* makes that promise an unending, visible, permanent reality.

> **Revelation 21:3 (NASB1995)**
> *"And I heard a loud voice from the throne, saying, 'Behold, the tabernacle of God is among men, and He will dwell among them, and they shall be His people, and God Himself will be among them.'"*

> **1 Corinthians 15:24-28 (NASB1995)**
> *"Then comes the end, when He hands over the kingdom to the God and Father, when He has abolished all rule and all authority and power. For He must reign until He has put all His enemies under His feet. The last enemy that will be abolished is death. For 'He has put all things in subjection under His feet.' But when He says, 'All things are put in subjection,' it is evident that He is excepted who put all things in subjection to Him. When all things are subjected to Him, then the Son Himself also will be subjected to the One who subjected all things to Him, so that God may be all in all."*

The Gog war of Ezekiel 38–39 is the Lord's final act of placing every enemy under the feet of Christ. Death follows. Judgment follows. And then — God is all in all.

That is where the book of Ezekiel is going. And it is where the whole of Scripture is going. The prophet who fell on his face before the glory of the Lord in Chapter 1 has now been shown — vision by vision, section by section — exactly how that glory will fill the earth as the waters cover the sea.

> **Habakkuk 2:14 (NASB1995)**
> *"For the earth will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD, as the waters cover the sea."*
