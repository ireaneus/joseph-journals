## Where We Stand in the Text

Before we enter Ezekiel 37, it is worth pausing to remember where we are in the book — and why it matters.

The Lord had already pronounced devastating judgment on Israel. The glory had departed the temple. Jerusalem had fallen. The people sat in exile, scattered, and the prevailing sense among them was that the story was over.

That is the voice of a people who believed God's covenant promises had failed — or worse, that Israel had forfeited them beyond any hope of recovery. The nation was not merely suffering. They had concluded that the story was over.

What the Lord does in Ezekiel 37 is answer that conclusion directly — not with comfort, not with sentiment, but with a demonstration of His sovereign power and His unbroken covenant faithfulness. The chapter is not primarily about what Israel felt. It is about what God does when His name and His word are at stake.

---

## What the Lord is Building Toward

To understand this chapter, we need to understand its place in the larger structure the Lord is revealing through Ezekiel from Chapter 33 onward.

God had made promises to Abraham, and later to David. Those promises were not abstract. They were specific, covenantal, and — to human eyes in the days of exile — seemingly impossible. They can be grouped into four areas:

- A king from the line of David to rule over His people
- An inheritance of land, free of competing claims, blessed with abundance
- Many descendants living in the land in peace and prosperity
- God Himself dwelling among His people

Each of these four covenantal promises receives extended attention in Chapters 33 through 48. Chapter 37 addresses the third — a people restored to peaceful inhabitation of the land. The structure is deliberate. The Lord is not offering comfort in the abstract. He is showing, section by section, that every promise He made to Abraham and David will be fulfilled.

> **Genesis 12:2-3 (NASB1995)**
> *"And I will make you a great nation, and I will bless you, and make your name great; and so you shall be a blessing; and I will bless those who bless you, and the one who curses you I will curse. And in you all the families of the earth will be blessed."*

> **Genesis 15:18 (NASB1995)**
> *"On that day the LORD made a covenant with Abram, saying, 'To your descendants I have given this land, from the river of Egypt as far as the great river, the river Euphrates.'"*

> **Genesis 22:16-17 (NASB1995)**
> *"By Myself I have sworn, declares the LORD, because you have done this thing and have not withheld your son, your only son, indeed I will greatly bless you, and I will greatly multiply your seed as the stars of the heavens and as the sand which is on the seashore; and your seed shall possess the gate of their enemies."*

> **Genesis 28:13-14 (NASB1995)**
> *"And behold, the LORD stood above it and said, 'I am the LORD, the God of your father Abraham and the God of Isaac; the land on which you lie, I will give it to you and to your descendants. Your descendants will also be like the dust of the earth, and you will spread out to the west and to the east and to the north and to the south; and in you and in your descendants shall all the families of the earth be blessed.'"*

These are not promises made to the Church. They are promises made to a specific people, concerning a specific land, with a specific king. Ezekiel 37 is the Lord's declaration that He will keep every word of them.

The explicit reason the Lord gives for acting is not Israel's merit but His own holy name:

> **Ezekiel 36:22-23 (NASB1995)**
> *"Therefore say to the house of Israel, 'Thus says the Lord GOD, "It is not for your sake, O house of Israel, that I am about to act, but for My holy name, which you have profaned among the nations where you went. I will vindicate the holiness of My great name which has been profaned among the nations, which you have profaned in their midst. Then the nations will know that I am the LORD," declares the Lord GOD, "when I prove Myself holy among you in their sight."'"*

That is the foundation of everything that follows. The restoration is grounded entirely in who God is, not in anything Israel has done or will do. That is the interpretive key that unlocks the rest of the chapter.

## The Valley of Dry Bones

> **Ezekiel 37:1-2 (NASB1995)**
> *"The hand of the LORD was upon me, and He brought me out by the Spirit of the LORD and set me down in the middle of the valley; and it was full of bones. He caused me to pass among them round about, and behold, there were very many on the surface of the valley; and lo, they were very dry."*

The vision the Lord gives Ezekiel is not a gentle picture. It is a valley of human bones — exposed, scattered, and very dry. The dryness matters. It signals not a recent death but a long one. Whatever life these bones once had, it is distant memory now.

Notice that the Lord walks Ezekiel through the valley before He speaks a word of explanation. Ezekiel is not given a summary. He is made to look — to take in the full extent of the desolation before the Lord reveals what He intends to do with it. This is the same valley Ezekiel had visited before, the plain where the glory of the Lord had first appeared to him at the beginning of his prophetic ministry:

> **Ezekiel 3:22-23 (NASB1995)**
> *"The hand of the LORD was on me there, and He said to me, 'Get up, go out to the plain, and there I will speak to you.' So I got up and went out to the plain; and behold, the glory of the LORD was standing there, like the glory which I saw by the river Chebar, and I fell on my face."*

The Lord brings Ezekiel back to the same ground — but now it is filled with bones. The place of the Lord's glory has become a valley of death. That contrast is intentional. And what the Lord is about to do there will reverse it entirely.

When the Lord asks Ezekiel the question, notice how the prophet answers:

> **Ezekiel 37:3 (NASB1995)**
> *"He said to me, 'Son of man, can these bones live?' And I answered, 'O Lord GOD, You know.'"*

Ezekiel does not say yes. He does not say no. He says: *You know.* That is not evasion. That is the posture of a servant who has learned where the limits of his own understanding end and the unlimited authority of God begins.

> **Romans 9:20 (NASB1995)**
> *"On the contrary, who are you, O man, who answers back to God? The thing molded will not say to the molder, 'Why did you make me like this,' will it?"*

The clay does not instruct the potter. Ezekiel defers — and in that deferral, he is positioned to receive what the Lord is about to reveal.

---

## Prophesy to the Bones

The Lord does not explain the vision before commanding Ezekiel to prophesy into it. He commands the speaking first.

> **Ezekiel 37:4-6 (NASB1995)**
> *"Again He said to me, 'Prophesy over these bones and say to them, "O dry bones, hear the word of the LORD." Thus says the Lord GOD to these bones, "Behold, I will cause breath to enter you that you may come to life. I will put sinews on you, make flesh grow back on you, cover you with skin and put breath in you that you may come alive; and you will know that I am the LORD."'"*

This is the pattern the Lord established from the beginning. He speaks, and life follows. It was true in Genesis 1, and it is true here. The prophetic word is not Ezekiel's opinion about the future of the nation. It is the authoritative declaration of what the Sovereign God has determined. Ezekiel speaks as a vessel through which the Lord's word goes forth, and the bones respond.

The role of the servant is faithfulness to what has been commanded — not addition, not omission:

> **Jeremiah 26:2 (NASB1995)**
> *"Thus says the LORD, 'Stand in the court of the LORD'S house, and speak to all the cities of Judah who have come to worship in the LORD'S house all the words that I have commanded you to speak to them. Do not omit a word!'"*

> **Ezekiel 37:7-8 (NASB1995)**
> *"So I prophesied as I was commanded; and as I prophesied, there was a noise, and behold, a rattling; and the bones came together, bone to its bone. And I looked, and behold, sinews were on them, and flesh grew and skin covered them; but there was no breath in them."*

The process happens in stages. That detail is not incidental. The restoration of what the Lord has promised unfolds over time, visibly and unmistakably, so that anyone watching will know that something is being reassembled by a power greater than the pieces themselves. Yet even when the bodies are formed, they remain without breath — structure without life, form without the Spirit.

> **Ezekiel 37:9-10 (NASB1995)**
> *"Then He said to me, 'Prophesy to the breath, prophesy, son of man, and say to the breath, "Thus says the Lord GOD, 'Come from the four winds, O breath, and breathe on these slain, that they come to life.'"' So I prophesied as He commanded me, and the breath came into them, and they came to life and stood on their feet, an exceedingly great army."*

The Hebrew word translated *breath* is *ruach* — the same word for *Spirit*. It appears eight times in this short passage. In Scripture's pattern, eight signals new beginnings. The Lord is restarting something. The same Spirit who moved over the face of the waters in Genesis 1 is the One who breathes life into what death had claimed. No human agency accomplishes this. The Lord does it by His word and by His Spirit alone.

## What the Vision Means

The Lord does not leave the interpretation to us.

> **Ezekiel 37:11-14 (NASB1995)**
> *"Then He said to me, 'Son of man, these bones are the whole house of Israel; behold, they say, "Our bones are dried up and our hope has perished. We are completely cut off." Therefore prophesy and say to them, "Thus says the Lord GOD, 'Behold, I will open your graves and cause you to come up out of your graves, My people; and I will bring you into the land of Israel. Then you will know that I am the LORD, when I have opened your graves and caused you to come up out of your graves, My people. I will put My Spirit within you and you will come to life, and I will place you on your own land. Then you will know that I, the LORD, have spoken and done it,'" declares the LORD.'"*

The bones are not individual Israelites scattered across history. The bones are the *whole house of Israel* — the nation itself, the collective covenantal people — in a state of apparent extinction. The nation had ceased to exist on the world stage. It had been cut off from the land.

This vision is not an allegory for spiritual regeneration in general — though the imagery illuminates that truth. The Lord's own interpretation is specific and national: the graves are the nations from which Israel has been dispersed, and the people coming up out of those graves are the Jewish people returning to the land of Israel in stages. That return has been visible over the past century — bones beginning to assemble. But the final breath, the national salvation of Israel, has not yet come. That awaits the return of Christ.

The Lord had already promised this new-covenant work of the Spirit in the chapter immediately preceding:

> **Ezekiel 36:26-27 (NASB1995)**
> *"Moreover, I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit within you; and I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh. I will put My Spirit within you and cause you to walk in My statutes, and you will be careful to observe My ordinances."*

And Jeremiah had prophesied the same, confirming this was always the Lord's intention for this specific people:

> **Jeremiah 31:33-34 (NASB1995)**
> *"But this is the covenant which I will make with the house of Israel after those days," declares the LORD, "I will put My law within them and on their heart I will write it; and I will be their God, and they shall be My people. They will not teach again, each man his neighbor and each man his brother, saying, 'Know the LORD,' for they will all know Me, from the least of them to the greatest of them," declares the LORD, "for I will forgive their iniquity, and their sin I will remember no more."*

This is not a promise made to the Church and applied to Israel secondarily. This is a promise made to the house of Israel — by name, in context, in continuity with the covenant the Lord made with their fathers.

## The Two Sticks

After the bones vision, the Lord gives Ezekiel a second acted sign.

> **Ezekiel 37:15-17 (NASB1995)**
> *"The word of the LORD came again to me saying, 'And you, son of man, take for yourself one stick and write on it, "For Judah and for the sons of Israel, his companions"; then take another stick and write on it, "For Joseph, the stick of Ephraim and all the house of Israel, his companions." Then join them for yourself one to another into one stick, that they may become one in your hand.'"*

After Solomon's death, the kingdom divided. The northern tribes — called Israel or Ephraim after their largest tribe — went one direction. Judah, the southern kingdom, went another. The northern kingdom was destroyed by Assyria. The southern kingdom was taken by Babylon. They had divergent histories, divergent exiles, and one never returned as a group.

The Lord takes two sticks and joins them into one. What was divided by sin and judgment will be reunited by the sovereign hand of God — not by political negotiation, not by human diplomacy, but by the Lord's decree alone.

> **Ezekiel 37:21-23 (NASB1995)**
> *"Say to them, 'Thus says the Lord GOD, "Behold, I will take the sons of Israel from among the nations where they have gone, and I will gather them from every side and bring them into their own land; and I will make them one nation in the land, on the mountains of Israel; and one king will be king for all of them; and they will no longer be two nations and no longer be divided into two kingdoms. They will no longer defile themselves with their idols, or with their detestable things, or with any of their transgressions; but I will deliver them from all their dwelling places in which they have sinned, and will cleanse them. And they will be My people, and I will be their God."'"*

The Lord is speaking of a literal people, a literal land, a literal reunification under a literal king. The text gives no warrant for allegory here. The Lord is not finished with Israel. He made promises to Abraham that He intends to keep — to the letter, in the land, with this people.

## My Servant David Will Be King

The restored, reunited nation will not be left without a shepherd. The Lord promises a specific King.

> **Ezekiel 37:24-25 (NASB1995)**
> *"My servant David will be king over them, and they will all have one shepherd; and they will walk in My ordinances and keep My statutes and observe them. They will live on the land that I gave to Jacob My servant, in which your fathers lived; and they will live on it, they, and their sons and their sons' sons, forever; and David My servant will be their prince forever."*

The Lord is speaking of the Messiah — the greater Son of David, the fulfillment of the Davidic covenant. The angel's announcement to Mary was not poetry. It was the literal fulfillment of what the prophets had declared for centuries:

> **Luke 1:32-33 (NASB1995)**
> *"He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High; and the Lord God will give Him the throne of His father David; and He will reign over the house of Jacob forever, and His kingdom will have no end."*

> **Jeremiah 23:5 (NASB1995)**
> *"'Behold, the days are coming,' declares the LORD, 'When I will raise up for David a righteous Branch; and He will reign as king and act wisely and do justice and righteousness in the land.'"*

> **Isaiah 9:6-7 (NASB1995)**
> *"For a child will be born to us, a son will be given to us; and the government will rest on His shoulders; and His name will be called Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Eternal Father, Prince of Peace. There will be no end to the increase of His government or of peace, on the throne of David and over his kingdom, to establish it and to uphold it with justice and righteousness from then on and forevermore. The zeal of the LORD of hosts will accomplish this."*

This is not a symbolic reign. It is a literal, physical, earthly reign from Jerusalem over a restored and reunited Israel during the millennial kingdom. The zeal of the LORD of hosts will accomplish it — not Israel's merit, not any human program.

## The Covenant of Peace

The chapter closes with one of the most significant theological foundations in the entire book — one that is essential for understanding what follows in Chapters 38 and 39.

> **Ezekiel 37:26-28 (NASB1995)**
> *"I will make a covenant of peace with them; it will be an everlasting covenant with them. And I will place them and multiply them, and will set My sanctuary in their midst forever. My dwelling place also will be with them; and I will be their God, and they will be My people. And the nations will know that I am the LORD who sanctifies Israel, when My sanctuary is in their midst forever."*

Three things must not be missed here.

First, the covenant of peace is *everlasting*. It is not conditional on Israel's ongoing faithfulness. The covenant-keeping God binds Himself by His own word — as He did with Abraham, with Noah, with David. His decrees do not fail.

> **Psalm 33:11 (NASB1995)**
> *"The counsel of the LORD stands forever, the plans of His heart from generation to generation."*

Second, the dwelling of God among His people is not temporary. *My sanctuary in their midst forever.* This is the destination the whole book is moving toward. The glory that departed in Ezekiel 10 will return. The Lord will dwell with His people — and that dwelling is the final answer to every question the exile raised about whether God had abandoned them.

Third, this covenant of peace is the direct contextual basis for the Gog invasion in Chapters 38-39. A covenant of peace only demonstrates its power when peace is threatened. That tension — promise made, promise tested, promise kept — is precisely what Chapters 38 and 39 are designed to show. Gog does not invade a weak or scattered Israel. Gog invades a restored, peaceful, unguarded Israel living securely in the land. And the Lord defeats the invader to prove Himself faithful to the covenant He made here in Chapter 37.

This is the consistent pattern of God's work — He makes the promise, He permits the test, and He keeps the promise — so that the nations and His people will know without ambiguity that He is the LORD.

> **Isaiah 46:9-10 (NASB1995)**
> *"Remember the former things long past, for I am God, and there is no other; I am God, and there is no one like Me, declaring the end from the beginning, and from ancient times things which have not been done, saying, 'My purpose will be established, and I will accomplish all My good pleasure.'"*

## What This Means Looking Forward

Ezekiel 37 is not a chapter about the past. The events it describes have not yet been fully accomplished. Israel was regathered to the land beginning in the last century — bones beginning to assemble, visibly, in stages — but the national salvation of Israel, the Spirit breathed into the nation, and the covenant of peace inaugurated under the reign of the Davidic King: these await the return of Christ.

That is why this chapter is foundational to Chapters 38 and 39. The Gog war does not occur against a dispersed or unbelieving Israel. It occurs against a restored, redeemed, peacefully dwelling Israel — a people fully inhabited by the Spirit, living under the everlasting covenant their God has made with them. The details of that coming war only make sense against this backdrop.

The same God who caused dry bones to rattle and assemble in a valley governs every detail of what is still to come. His word does not return to Him empty.

> **Isaiah 55:11 (NASB1995)**
> *"So will My word be which goes forth from My mouth; it will not return to Me empty, without accomplishing what I desire, and without succeeding in the matter for which I sent it."*

> **Revelation 11:15 (NASB1995)**
> *"The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of His Christ; and He will reign forever and ever."*
