## Introduction

There is something in us that is always trading. We trade time for comfort, truth for acceptance, holiness for convenience, and eternal treasure for things that glitter for a moment and disappear.

It is like a child panning for gold and holding up a shiny rock with delight, convinced a treasure has been found. To the child, it looks precious. To the one who knows better, it is only a worthless trinket.

We do the same thing spiritually.

God has set before us riches beyond measure in Christ, and yet our hearts are often drawn toward lesser things. We become distracted by the approval of people, earthly success, temporary pleasures, and even religious performance. We are too easily impressed by what sparkles now, while forgetting what God has already given us in His Son.

That is why Paul begins Ephesians not with a command, but with praise.

Before he tells believers what to do, he tells them what God has done. Before he speaks of the Christian walk, he lifts our eyes to the God who has already blessed us. And he does so in language so rich and full that it overwhelms the soul with divine grace.

Ephesians 1:3–14 is a mountain peak of praise. It shows us that the whole work of salvation is from God, in Christ, and for the praise of His glory.

## Main Point

The central truth of this passage is this:

**In Christ, God has given His people every spiritual blessing through the full saving work of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit, to the praise of His glory.**

Paul begins in verse 3:

> *“Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who has blessed us with every spiritual blessing in the heavenly places in Christ.”*

This is the fountainhead of the entire passage. Paul blesses God because God has blessed His people. And these are not partial blessings, temporary blessings, or uncertain blessings. They are **every spiritual blessing**. Nothing necessary for our salvation is missing. Nothing essential to our standing before God has been withheld.

These blessings are spiritual, not because they are unreal, but because they are given by the Holy Spirit, secured in Christ, and rooted in the heavenly places. They are greater than earthly wealth, deeper than passing comfort, and more lasting than anything this world can offer.

Paul then unfolds these blessings through the work of the triune God.

### The Father planned our salvation

Verses 4–6 show that the Father chose His people in Christ before the foundation of the world. This means salvation did not begin with us. It did not begin with our decision, our worthiness, or our seeking after God. It began in the eternal purpose of the Father.

He chose us, not because we were holy, but that we would become holy. He predestined us to adoption, not because we deserved sonship, but because He set His love upon us in Christ.

This is not cold theology. This is warm, worshipful truth. The believer is not an afterthought. The believer is not a spiritual accident. The believer is loved with an eternal love and brought into the family of God according to His kind intention.

And Paul says all of this is “to the praise of the glory of His grace.”

Grace is glorious because it is undeserved. If salvation began in us, then praise would return to us. But because salvation begins in God, all praise belongs to Him.

### The Son accomplished our salvation

Verses 7–12 move from the Father’s eternal plan to the Son’s redeeming work in history.

> *“In Him we have redemption through His blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of His grace.”*

Redemption means we were not free. We were in bondage to sin, guilty before God, and unable to rescue ourselves. But Christ did not merely make salvation possible. He purchased it. He secured it. He accomplished it through His blood.

Our forgiveness is not thin or reluctant. It is according to the riches of His grace. God does not forgive sparingly. He forgives lavishly in Christ.

In Jesus we also receive an inheritance. We are not only rescued from wrath, but brought into blessing. We are not merely pardoned criminals; we are welcomed heirs.

This destroys pride and gives assurance. If Christ has redeemed us by His blood, then our standing with God rests on His finished work, not our fluctuating emotions or inconsistent performance.

Again, Paul says this is *“to the praise of His glory.”*

### The Spirit applied and secured our salvation

Verses 13–14 bring us to the work of the Holy Spirit.

After hearing the word of truth, the gospel of salvation, and believing in Christ, believers are sealed with the Holy Spirit of promise.

The Spirit is God’s mark of ownership. He is the guarantee of our inheritance. He is the pledge that what God began, He will complete.

This means the Christian life is not sustained by human strength. The same God who planned salvation and accomplished salvation now preserves His people by His Spirit.

The Spirit does not merely visit the believer. He seals the believer. He is the down payment of coming glory, the assurance that the inheritance is real, and the promise that full redemption is still to come.

And once more, Paul ends with the same great aim: *“to the praise of His glory.”*

## Application

This passage does not merely teach doctrine. It corrects the heart.

We are reminded here that we often live as though we are spiritually poor, when in Christ we are spiritually rich. We chase shiny stones while neglecting heavenly treasure. We become consumed with what fades, while forgetting what is eternal.

So how should we respond?

First, we should respond with worship.

Paul begins with “Blessed be the God and Father.” Right doctrine should lead to doxology. Election should lead to praise. Redemption should lead to praise. Sealing by the Spirit should lead to praise. If these truths only make us argumentative, then we have not yet understood them rightly.

Second, we should respond with humility.

There is no room in this passage for self-congratulation. The Father chose, the Son redeemed, and the Spirit sealed. Salvation is of the Lord from beginning to end. That humbles us. We did not rescue ourselves. We did not contribute merit to the work of Christ. We are recipients of grace.

Third, we should respond with assurance.

If salvation rests in the purpose of the Father, the blood of the Son, and the seal of the Spirit, then the believer has deep reason for confidence. Our hope is not built on the sand of our feelings, but on the rock of God’s finished work in Christ.

Fourth, we should respond with holiness.

Paul says we were chosen that we would be holy and blameless before Him. These blessings are not an excuse for careless living. They are the foundation for grateful obedience. The Christian does not pursue holiness to earn blessing; the Christian pursues holiness because he has already been blessed in Christ.

Finally, we should stop trading eternal treasure for worthless trinkets.

The world still sparkles. Sin still makes its promises. Lesser things still compete for our hearts. But Paul calls us back to reality. In Christ we already possess every spiritual blessing. Why should we live as beggars when God has made us heirs? Why should we chase what cannot last when we have been given what can never be taken away?

The answer to a wandering heart is not found in trying harder to love God. It is found in seeing more clearly what God has already done for us in Christ.

So let us bless the God who has blessed us.

Let us praise the Father who chose us, the Son who redeemed us, and the Spirit who sealed us.

And let us live in the joy, humility, assurance, and holiness that flow from this great truth:

**In Christ, God has given His people every spiritual blessing to the praise of His glory.**
