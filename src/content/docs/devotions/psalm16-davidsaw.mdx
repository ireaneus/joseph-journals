> **Psalm 16:10 (NASB1995)**
> *"For You will not abandon my soul to Sheol; nor will You allow Your Holy One to undergo decay."*

---

## The Man Who Wrote It

David wrote Psalm 16 as a man who knew what it was to take refuge in God. The opening is not poetry for the page — it is a man talking to God in distress. *"Preserve me, O God, for I take refuge in You"* **(v.1)**. He goes on to confess God as his portion, his cup, his inheritance. He has set the Lord before him. He trusts he will not be shaken.

Then he arrives at verses 10 and 11, and the language rises higher than one life can hold.

David had not escaped Sheol in that literal sense. He died. His body was buried. His tomb was still visible a thousand years later on the day Peter stood up to preach.

So what was David doing? He was writing what he could not fully see.

---

## The Man Who Named It

Fifty days after the resurrection, Peter stands in Jerusalem and quotes this psalm. He does not quote it as a general comfort. He quotes it as a case.

> **Acts 2:29–31 (NASB1995)**
> *"Brethren, I may confidently say to you regarding the patriarch David that he both died and was buried, and his tomb is with us to this day. And so, because he was a prophet and knew that God had sworn to him with an oath to seat one of his descendants on his throne, he looked ahead and spoke of the resurrection of the Christ, that He was neither abandoned to Hades, nor did His flesh suffer decay."*

Peter's argument is simple. David died. David's body decayed. Verse 10 cannot be about David — not in its fullest sense. David was a prophet, and prophets sometimes write further than they can see. He knew the oath God had sworn him. He looked ahead. He spoke of the one who would come from his line, and in whom the promise would find its only complete fulfillment.

The tomb could not hold Christ. His flesh did not see decay. What David wrote in confidence — trusting that God would not abandon him permanently — was true beyond what David knew. It was true in a way that only the resurrection could explain.

---

## The Path He Walked

Verse 10 is the burial. Verse 11 is what came after it.

> **Psalm 16:11 (NASB1995)**
> *"You will make known to me the path of life; in Your presence is fullness of joy; in Your right hand there are pleasures forever."*

The Father made known to the Son the path through death into life. The path did not go around the grave — it went through it. And what waited on the other side was not merely survival. It was fullness of joy. Pleasures at the right hand of God forever.

Christ now sits there. The writer of Hebrews says He endured the cross — despising its shame — for the joy set before Him (Hebrews 12:2 NASB1995). That joy was not incidental. It was the destination. The path of life led through death to the presence of the Father, and there it opened into something no human language can fully carry.

> **Hebrews 12:2**  
> *looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith, who for the joy that was set before him endured the cross, despising the shame, and is seated at the right hand of the throne of God.*

Fullness of joy. Pleasures forever.

> **1 Corinthians 15:20-23**  
> *But in fact Christ has been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep. For as by a man came death, by a man has come also the resurrection of the dead. For as in Adam all die, so also in Christ shall all be made alive. But each in his own order: Christ the firstfruits, then at his coming those who belong to Christ.*

David wrote it. Peter explained it. Christ walked it.

What David glimpsed ahead and could not fully see, we now see in the light of an empty tomb and an ascended King. The psalm has not changed. But we read it now from the other side of the resurrection, and we know the name of the one it was about.
