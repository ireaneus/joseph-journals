### **Intro – Sermon Series on Revelation**

Today, I’m beginning a new sermon series on the final book of Holy Scripture: *The Revelation of Jesus Christ*.

I imagine I’m speaking mostly to Christians today—but I also hope there are a few listening who are still exploring Christianity or have questions about it. And now that I’ve said “Revelation,” I’ll bet I’ve piqued your interest. Whether you're a believer or not, it seems everyone has *some* curiosity about this book.

So let me ask the believers in the room:
**Wouldn't you like to know more about the Savior you've placed your faith and trust in?**

And for those who don’t yet know Jesus:
**Wouldn’t you want to find out whether what we believe is actually true—and who this Jesus really is that we keep talking about?**

Let’s be honest: Revelation is often viewed as a confusing or even intimidating book. It’s been the subject of endless speculation, misinterpretation, and sometimes fear. But that was never the purpose of the book.

#### *Misconception #1: Revelation is not a code to be cracked, a mystery novel, or a roadmap to the end times.*

While it speaks of future events, the real subject is plainly stated in the opening words:

> *“The Revelation of Jesus Christ…”*

This book is not primarily about beasts, seals, bowls, or judgments.

#### *It’s about Jesus. Who He is. His Kingdom. His plan of redemption and restoration.*

Now, teaching the last book of the Bible without understanding the rest of it can feel like joining a book club and only reading the last chapter—it’s easy to get confused or miss the meaning. That’s why, throughout this series, we’ll not only walk through the 22 chapters of John's vision, but we’ll also take time to look back—into Genesis, Exodus, Deuteronomy, Leviticus, Ruth, Job, Psalms, the Prophets, the Gospels, and the Epistles.

In fact, 28 books of the Old Testament are directly referenced in Revelation—even if not quoted word-for-word. This is not a standalone book; it’s the *culmination* of everything God has been revealing from the beginning.

This is a divine unveiling, a disclosure originating with God the Father, given to Jesus Christ, and communicated through His angel to His bond-servant John. The ultimate source is God, who is eternal, infinite, and unchanging, the one essence in three distinct persons: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. He is the sovereign Creator and exercises supreme authority over all creation.
The purpose of this revelation is clear: "to show to His bond-servants, the things which must soon take place". It's a message intended for those who serve God. It deals with future events, things that are soon to occur, revealing the culmination of God's eternal plan. Our sources remind us that God governs all events according to His eternal plan, upholding all things in heaven and on earth, and that His decrees are eternal, unchangeable, and encompass all that will ever happen. Nothing is beyond His control. The Book of Revelation unfolds the final stages of this sovereign providence.
Verse 2 tells us about the human recipient of this message: John, "who testified to the word of God and to the testimony of Jesus Christ, everything that he saw". John is bearing witness to the divine word and the specific revelation given to him by and about Jesus Christ.
Now, consider the profound blessing pronounced in verse 3: "Blessed is the one who reads, and those who hear the words of the prophecy and keep the things which are written in it; for the time is near". There is a distinct blessing associated with engaging with this prophecy. This blessing is for those who actively read it, those who hear its words, and crucially, those who keep or follow the things written in it. It's not merely intellectual understanding, but obedient response that brings the blessing. The urgency is underscored by the statement, "for the time is near".
This book is the final chapter in God's written revelation to humanity, the Bible. It builds upon the preceding 65 books, bringing characters, themes, and plot elements introduced earlier to their ultimate conclusion. As one source suggests, understanding Revelation properly requires a foundational understanding of the entire Bible.
The central message of the entire Bible, from Genesis to Revelation, is the revelation of God’s Kingdom, the redemption of His people, and the glory of God—all accomplished through the person and work of Jesus Christ. Revelation culminates this narrative. We see the glorious rule of Christ over all creation, the consummation of God’s Kingdom, the final judgment, the second coming of the Messiah, and the ushering in of eternity, where the redeemed worship God in the New Heaven and New Earth.
Revelation 1:8 gives us one of the majestic titles of God found throughout the book and hinted at here in the introduction: "I am the Alpha and the Omega," says the Lord God, "who is and who was and who is to come, the Almighty." This title underscores God's eternal nature, His comprehensive determination of all things, His sovereignty over all creation, and His ultimate authority from beginning to end.
So, as we approach the Book of Revelation, let us remember:
•
It is God's own revelation, given through Christ.
•
Its purpose is to show us future events which are soon to take place.
•
There is a blessing for those who read, hear, and obey its message.
•
It is the culmination of God's redemptive plan through Jesus Christ.
•
It reveals the sovereignty and glory of the Almighty God.
Let us come to this study not with fear, but with eager anticipation to see how God's eternal plan unfolds, to better understand Christ's victorious reign, and to be encouraged to live lives worthy of the calling we have received, keeping the things written in this prophecy.
